# Module 9: Understanding Neurodivergence in Schools

## Purpose

This module introduces neurodivergence as a default lens for understanding behaviour in schools. It helps staff recognise that behaviour often reflects neurological difference and environmental mismatch, not defiance or choice. It does not train staff to diagnose; it supports them to consider neurodivergence in every scenario and to respond in ways that match how children's brains work.

## Intended Audience

All staff (teachers, support staff, leaders)

## Time Range

45–60 minutes (flexible)

## What Participants Should Leave With

1. **What is neurodivergence?** — Autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia, dyscalculia, Tourette's, PDA (Pathological Demand Avoidance) as different ways brains work, not deficits.
2. **The neurodivergent–neurotypical communication gap** — Why literal interpretation, processing delay, and different social cues lead to misread behaviour.
3. **Behaviour as communication — always** — Every behaviour carries information about need, overwhelm, or environment.
4. **Meltdown vs tantrum — how to tell** — Tantrum: goal-directed, audience-aware. Meltdown: overwhelm, thinking brain offline, no "choice."
5. **Masking — what it costs** — Performing neurotypicality at enormous cognitive and emotional cost; collapse when capacity is exceeded.
6. **The spiky profile** — Why "but they CAN do X" is misleading; uneven development across domains.
7. **PDA — when every demand feels like a threat** — Anxiety-driven need for control; consequences escalate rather than reduce behaviour.
8. **Sensory processing — the invisible curriculum** — Over-responsive, under-responsive, sensory-seeking; lighting, noise, smell, proximity, transitions.
9. **Girls, race, and poverty — who gets missed** — Significant under-diagnosis; behaviour attributed to character or background instead of neurology.
10. **Reasonable adjustments — not special treatment** — Equal access; reduce demands, movement breaks, visual supports, processing time, environmental changes.

## Session Shape

### 45-Minute Variant

**Reading and orientation (25 minutes):**
- What is neurodivergence? (plain language)
- Behaviour as communication; meltdown vs tantrum
- Sensory environment and spiky profile

**Reflection and application (20 minutes):**
- Optional: consider one child (without naming) whose behaviour you might re-read through a neurodivergence lens
- No diagnosis; no personal disclosure

### 60-Minute Variant

**Reading and orientation (35 minutes):**
- All sections 1–10 above
- PDA and masking; who gets missed; reasonable adjustments

**Reflection and application (25 minutes):**
- Optional reflection on one environmental change that could support more children
- Quiz-style check: "Which of these is a meltdown, which a tantrum?" (scenario-based)

## Optional Activities

### Activity 1: Meltdown vs Tantrum (5 minutes)

**Task:** Read two short scenarios and identify which describes a meltdown and which a tantrum. Consider: What would a safe, non-escalating response be in each?

### Activity 2: Sensory Environment (5 minutes)

**Task:** List three aspects of your setting (noise, lighting, transitions, etc.) that might overwhelm a sensory-sensitive child. What one small change could reduce load?

### Activity 3: Language Check (5 minutes)

**Task:** Rewrite "He's attention-seeking" and "She's refusing to work" using neurodivergence-aware language (connection-seeking, unable/unwilling to meet demand, consider can't vs won't).

## Boundaries

- This module does **not** train staff to diagnose. Use "consider whether…" and "it may be worth exploring…" not "this child has…"
- Frame neurodivergence as a **lens**, not a label.
- Unidentified neurodivergence is more common than identified — include this lens even when no diagnosis is known.
- Reasonable adjustments benefit many children; they are not "special treatment."

## Quiz

**Section 1 — What is neurodivergence?**

1. Neurodivergence is best described as:
   - A) A medical disorder that needs treatment ← Wrong. Neurodivergence is neurological variation, not pathology.
   - B) A difference in how the brain processes information ← Correct. It's variation, not deficit.
   - C) Something children grow out of ← Wrong. Neurodivergence is lifelong.
   - D) Only autism and ADHD ← Wrong. Includes dyslexia, dyspraxia, dyscalculia, Tourette's, PDA, and more.

2. "Reasonable adjustments" for neurodivergent children are:
   - A) Special treatment that other children don't get ← Wrong. They are equal access so the child can participate.
   - B) Equal access so the child can learn and participate ← Correct. Not special treatment — leveling the playing field.
   - C) Only for children with a formal diagnosis ← Wrong. Unidentified neurodivergence is common; include the lens even without diagnosis.
   - D) Optional and up to the teacher ← Wrong. They support access and are part of inclusive practice.

**Section 2 — Communication gap**

3. A child who takes things very literally may be:
   - A) Being difficult on purpose ← Wrong. Literal interpretation is a common neurodivergent trait, not defiance.
   - B) Showing a neurotypical communication style ← Wrong. Literal interpretation is often associated with neurodivergent processing.
   - C) Processing language differently — consider clarity and avoiding idioms ← Correct. Adjust how you give instructions.
   - D) Need to be corrected until they understand ← Wrong. Correction doesn't change neurology; clarity and adjustment do.

4. "Processing delay" in neurodivergent children means:
   - A) They are less intelligent ← Wrong. Processing speed is unrelated to intelligence.
   - B) They may need more time to hear, process, and respond — e.g. 10-second rule ← Correct. Give time before repeating or escalating.
   - C) They should be given easier work ← Wrong. The issue is time and environment, not lowering expectations inappropriately.
   - D) They cannot learn ← Wrong. They learn differently; support the processing, not the label.

**Section 3 — Behaviour as communication**

5. "Behaviour as communication" means:
   - A) We should let children do whatever they want ← Wrong. We respond to the need behind the behaviour, with boundaries.
   - B) Every behaviour carries information about need, overwhelm, or environment ← Correct. Our job is to read and respond to need.
   - C) Only some behaviours communicate ← Wrong. All behaviour communicates something; we may not yet understand it.
   - D) We should ask the child to explain in words ← Wrong. Many children cannot articulate in the moment; we infer and support.

6. When a child is "connection-seeking," the best response is:
   - A) Ignore so we don't reinforce it ← Wrong. Connection-seeking indicates unmet need; ignoring can increase distress.
   - B) Offer connection in a safe, predictable way while maintaining boundaries ← Correct. Meet the need, don't punish it.
   - C) Tell them to stop attention-seeking ← Wrong. "Attention-seeking" is blame language; reframe as connection-seeking.
   - D) Refer to a therapist ← Wrong. First response is relational and environmental; refer if needed after that.

**Section 4 — Meltdown vs tantrum**

7. A key difference between a meltdown and a tantrum is:
   - A) A tantrum is louder ← Wrong. Volume doesn't distinguish them.
   - B) A meltdown has no goal — the child has lost the ability to regulate ← Correct. A tantrum is goal-directed (the child wants something). A meltdown is overwhelm.
   - C) Only autistic children have meltdowns ← Wrong. Any child can experience a meltdown under sufficient stress.
   - D) You should ignore both ← Wrong. Ignoring a meltdown increases distress.

8. During a meltdown, the best immediate response is:
   - A) Reason with the child so they understand ← Wrong. The thinking brain is offline; reasoning won't work and can escalate.
   - B) Reduce sensory load, ensure safety, wait for the storm to pass ← Correct. Co-regulate and protect; process later.
   - C) Apply a consequence so they learn ← Wrong. Consequences during meltdown teach nothing and can retraumatise.
   - D) Move the child to isolation ← Wrong. Moving can escalate; reduce demand and stimulation in place where safe.

**Section 5 — Masking**

9. "Masking" in neurodivergent children means:
   - A) They are lying about their needs ← Wrong. Masking is performing neurotypicality to fit in; it's exhausting, not deceit.
   - B) Performing neurotypicality at enormous cognitive and emotional cost ← Correct. It can lead to burnout when capacity is exceeded.
   - C) They don't need support because they cope well ← Wrong. "Coping" may be masking; collapse often follows.
   - D) Only girls mask ← Wrong. Anyone can mask; it's often more observed in girls because of social expectations.

10. A child who "was fine until half term" and then stops attending may be:
    - A) Being lazy ← Wrong. Sudden collapse often indicates masking exhaustion or burnout.
    - B) Experiencing autistic burnout or masking collapse ← Correct. "Fine" often meant masking; capacity ran out.
    - C) Only stressed about exams ← Partially; but complete shutdown in a previously high-functioning child warrants dual-lens consideration.
    - D) Manipulating to stay home ← Wrong. Manipulation requires capacity; collapse is loss of capacity.

**Section 6 — Spiky profile**

11. A "spiky profile" means:
    - A) The child is inconsistent and needs to try harder ← Wrong. Spiky means uneven development across domains, not lack of effort.
    - B) The child may be advanced in some areas and significantly behind in others ← Correct. Don't judge the whole child by their highest ability.
    - C) The child has a behaviour problem ← Wrong. It's a description of development, not a behaviour label.
    - D) We should focus only on their weaknesses ← Wrong. Build on strengths and support needs; don't define by one domain.

12. "But they CAN do X" is misleading because:
    - A) It proves they're choosing not to do Y ← Wrong. Capacity in one context doesn't transfer to all contexts.
    - B) Capacity in one context (e.g. high interest) doesn't mean capacity in another (e.g. low interest, high demand) ← Correct. Context and neurology matter.
    - C) They are lazy ← Wrong. This is a neurotypical assumption that ignores neurological variation.
    - D) We should remove support ← Wrong. Support should match need across contexts.

**Section 7 — PDA**

13. For a child with a PDA profile, traditional consequences (e.g. sanctions) often:
    - A) Improve behaviour over time ← Wrong. For PDA, demands (including consequences) trigger threat; behaviour often escalates.
    - B) Escalate behaviour because they add more demand and trigger anxiety ← Correct. Reduce demand, use indirect language, offer choices.
    - C) Work if applied consistently ← Wrong. Consistency with the wrong approach doesn't make it right for that neurology.
    - D) Are the only way to set boundaries ← Wrong. Boundaries can be maintained with low-demand, predictable approaches.

14. "Indirect language" for PDA might sound like:
    - A) "Do it now." ← Wrong. Direct demand.
    - B) "I wonder if we could try…" or "When you're ready, you might…" ← Correct. Reduces perceived demand.
    - C) "If you don't, you'll lose break." ← Wrong. That's a consequence/demand.
    - D) "You have to." ← Wrong. Direct demand.

**Section 8 — Sensory processing**

15. A child who covers their ears in assembly may be:
    - A) Disrespectful ← Wrong. They may be over-responsive to noise; it can be painful.
    - B) Over-responsive to sound — offer ear defenders, quiet space, or reduce noise ← Correct. Sensory need, not behaviour choice.
    - C) Only doing it for attention ← Wrong. Reframe as communication of need.
    - D) Fine and should stay in assembly ← Wrong. Forcing exposure can increase distress and trauma.

16. "Sensory environment" includes:
    - A) Only noise ← Wrong. Also lighting, smell, proximity, transitions, crowding.
    - B) Lighting, noise, smell, proximity, transitions, unstructured time ← Correct. All can overwhelm or support.
    - C) Only what the child says ← Wrong. Many children can't articulate sensory overload.
    - D) Nothing we can change ← Wrong. We can adjust environment to reduce load.

**Section 9 — Who gets missed**

17. Girls and AFAB children are often under-diagnosed for autism/ADHD because:
    - A) They don't have autism/ADHD ← Wrong. They present differently and mask more; diagnostic criteria were built on male presentations.
    - B) They may mask more and present differently; criteria were historically based on male presentations ← Correct. Stay curious; don't assume.
    - C) They are less affected ← Wrong. They are affected; they're often missed.
    - D) Schools don't need to consider it ← Wrong. Schools should consider the lens for all children.

18. Behaviour attributed to "character" or "background" without considering neurodivergence:
    - A) Is usually correct ← Wrong. It can hide unidentified neurodivergence, especially in marginalised groups.
    - B) Can hide unidentified neurodivergence — always include the lens ← Correct. Single-story attribution misses need.
    - C) Is the school's job to fix ← Wrong. School's job is to support and refer appropriately, not to fix character.
    - D) Doesn't matter for support ← Wrong. Wrong attribution leads to wrong support.

**Section 10 — Reasonable adjustments**

19. Movement breaks for an ADHD child are:
    - A) A reward for good behaviour ← Wrong. They're a reasonable adjustment for regulation.
    - B) A reasonable adjustment — movement supports regulation for many ADHD brains ← Correct. Not a privilege to be removed.
    - C) Unnecessary if the child tries ← Wrong. Trying doesn't change neurology.
    - D) Only for young children ← Wrong. All ages can need movement for regulation.

20. "Reduce demands" in a crisis means:
    - A) Give in to the child ← Wrong. It means reduce cognitive/sensory/emotional load so the child can regulate — not abandon boundaries.
    - B) Reduce cognitive, sensory, and emotional load so the child can regulate ← Correct. Then re-engage when calm.
    - C) Remove all expectations forever ← Wrong. It's in-the-moment support, not permanent removal.
    - D) Let the child do nothing ← Wrong. It's targeted reduction, not total removal of structure.
