# Module 10: Trauma-Informed Practice in Schools

## Purpose

This module deepens understanding of trauma as a default lens in schools. It supports staff to recognise stress and survival responses (fight, flight, freeze, fawn), to prioritise felt safety, and to respond with PACE (Playfulness, Acceptance, Curiosity, Empathy). It does not train staff to treat or diagnose trauma; it helps them create conditions that support every child, including those who have experienced adversity.

## Intended Audience

All staff (teachers, support staff, leaders)

## Time Range

45–60 minutes (flexible)

## What Participants Should Leave With

1. **What is trauma?** — Single event, complex, developmental, relational, systemic. "What happened to you" not "what's wrong with you."
2. **The nervous system: fight, flight, freeze, fawn** — What each looks like in a classroom; aggression, avoidance, shutdown, people-pleasing as survival responses.
3. **ACEs and beyond** — Why the ACE score doesn't tell the whole story; everyday relational trauma (bullying, rejection, loss, humiliation, moving schools).
4. **Attachment styles in the classroom** — What each child may need from you; secure base, predictability, repair after rupture.
5. **The PACE approach (Dan Hughes)** — Playfulness, Acceptance, Curiosity, Empathy — connection before correction.
6. **"What happened to you?" not "What's wrong with you?"** — Paradigm shift from pathology to context.
7. **Felt safety** — Why physical safety isn't enough; emotional safety, predictability, and trust.
8. **Co-regulation before self-regulation** — You can't teach calm to a terrified child; your calm supports their regulation.
9. **Secondary and vicarious trauma** — Why your own wellbeing matters; tag out when dysregulated.
10. **Care-experienced children** — What school means when home keeps changing; attachment disruption, loss, trauma of the care system itself.

## Session Shape

### 45-Minute Variant

**Reading and orientation (25 minutes):**
- What is trauma? Fight, flight, freeze, fawn in the classroom
- Felt safety and co-regulation; PACE in brief

**Reflection and application (20 minutes):**
- Optional: one PACE-style phrase you could use instead of a corrective phrase
- No personal disclosure

### 60-Minute Variant

**Reading and orientation (35 minutes):**
- All sections 1–10
- Care-experienced children; secondary trauma and self-care

**Reflection and application (25 minutes):**
- Optional reflection on "What would a compassionate, curious response look like if I assumed the BEST about why this child is behaving this way?"
- Quiz-style: match behaviour to possible nervous system state (fight/flight/freeze/fawn)

## Optional Activities

### Activity 1: PACE in One Sentence (5 minutes)

**Task:** Take a common corrective sentence ("Stop shouting and sit down") and rewrite it using naming emotion, curiosity, or choice (e.g. "You seem really wound up. I wonder if you need a minute. You can stand by your chair or sit — I'll wait.")

### Activity 2: Felt Safety (5 minutes)

**Task:** List two things that might make a child feel emotionally unsafe in your setting even when physically safe. What one change could increase felt safety?

### Activity 3: Co-Regulation (5 minutes)

**Task:** "Regulate yourself first — your calm is their calm." What do you do when you notice you're becoming frustrated or rigid? (Pause, breathe, tag out.)

## Boundaries

- Do **not** assume you know what the trauma is. Many children have undisclosed trauma; some have complex trauma with no single "event."
- Frame trauma as "what happened to this child" not "what's wrong with this child."
- Behaviours that look problematic in school may have been survival strategies elsewhere. They are adaptive, not pathological.
- This module does not replace safeguarding training. Disclosure of abuse or neglect → immediate DSL referral.

## Quiz

**Section 1 — What is trauma?**

1. Trauma-informed practice is best described as:
   - A) Treating traumatised children ← Wrong. We don't treat; we create conditions that support all children, including those who have experienced adversity.
   - B) Recognising the impact of stress and adversity and designing environments that reduce harm and increase safety ← Correct. Environment and response, not diagnosis or treatment.
   - C) Identifying which children have trauma ← Wrong. We don't diagnose; we assume any child might be carrying stress and design accordingly.
   - D) Therapy in the classroom ← Wrong. No therapeutic techniques; safe, predictable, responsive environments.

2. "What happened to you?" not "What's wrong with you?" means:
   - A) We need to find out their trauma history ← Wrong. We don't need the story; we need to respond to need and create safety.
   - B) We shift from pathologising the child to considering context and experience ← Correct. Behaviour makes sense in context.
   - C) We blame their parents ← Wrong. We don't blame; we consider context without assigning blame.
   - D) We don't hold boundaries ← Wrong. We hold boundaries with compassion and curiosity.

**Section 2 — Fight, flight, freeze, fawn**

3. A child who is excessively compliant, always agrees with adults, and never expresses their own needs may be showing:
   - A) Good behaviour ← Wrong. Over-compliance can be a fawn response — the child has learned that their safety depends on keeping adults happy.
   - B) A fawn trauma response ← Correct. Fawning is a survival strategy. The child suppresses their own needs to avoid conflict or punishment.
   - C) That they don't need any support ← Wrong. These children are often the most overlooked; their distress is invisible.
   - D) Effective parenting ← Wrong. This pattern can indicate the child doesn't feel safe expressing disagreement.

4. "Freeze" response might look like:
   - A) Running away ← Wrong. That's flight.
   - B) Shutdown, dissociation, "zoning out," inability to respond ← Correct. The body goes still when fight/flight isn't possible.
   - C) Aggression ← Wrong. That's fight.
   - D) People-pleasing ← Wrong. That's fawn.

**Section 3 — ACEs and beyond**

5. The ACE score:
   - A) Tells us exactly what support a child needs ← Wrong. It's a broad indicator; it doesn't capture all adversity or individual response.
   - B) Doesn't tell the whole story — consider everyday relational trauma too (bullying, loss, humiliation, moves) ← Correct. ACEs are one lens; context is wider.
   - C) Is required before we respond with trauma-informed practice ← Wrong. We don't need a score; we respond to need and create safety for all.
   - D) Replaces the need for curiosity ← Wrong. Stay curious; the score is a prompt, not a label.

6. Everyday relational trauma includes:
   - A) Only abuse and neglect ← Wrong. Also bullying, rejection, academic humiliation, loss, parental separation, moving schools.
   - B) Bullying, rejection, academic humiliation, loss, parental separation, moving schools ← Correct. These shape the nervous system too.
   - C) Only what the child discloses ← Wrong. Many children don't disclose; we create safety regardless.
   - D) Nothing schools can address ← Wrong. Schools can create predictability, repair, and felt safety.

**Section 4 — Attachment**

7. A child who pushes away and then seeks closeness may be:
   - A) Manipulating ← Wrong. Reframe: testing whether the adult will stay; attachment-seeking.
   - B) Showing insecure attachment — testing whether the relationship is safe ← Correct. They need consistent, predictable presence.
   - C) Just moody ← Wrong. This pattern often reflects attachment history.
   - D) Need to be taught a lesson ← Wrong. They need safety and connection, not consequence.

8. For attachment-seeking behaviour, the best response is:
   - A) Withdraw relationship until they behave ← Wrong. Removing relationship as consequence damages trust further.
   - B) Offer consistent, predictable presence and repair after rupture ← Correct. Safety is built over time.
   - C) Refer only — we can't help ← Wrong. We can create conditions; refer when appropriate alongside.
   - D) Give them whatever they want ← Wrong. Boundaries and consistency support attachment; permissiveness doesn't build security.

**Section 5 — PACE**

9. PACE stands for:
   - A) Punishment, Accountability, Correction, Expectation ← Wrong.
   - B) Playfulness, Acceptance, Curiosity, Empathy ← Correct. Dan Hughes; connection before correction.
   - C) Plan, Act, Check, Evaluate ← Wrong.
   - D) Professional, Accurate, Clear, Evidence-based ← Wrong.

10. "Curiosity" in PACE might sound like:
    - A) "Why did you do that?" ← Wrong. That can sound accusatory.
    - B) "I wonder if…" or "What was that like for you?" ← Correct. Wondering, not interrogating.
    - C) "You know that was wrong." ← Wrong. That's correction, not curiosity.
    - D) "Don't do it again." ← Wrong. That's command, not curiosity.

**Section 6 — What happened to you**

11. We use "what happened to you" language to:
    - A) Get the child to tell us their trauma ← Wrong. We don't need the story to respond well.
    - B) Shift from "what's wrong with you" so we consider context and reduce shame ← Correct. Paradigm shift, not information gathering.
    - C) Report to safeguarding ← Wrong. Safeguarding has its own procedures; this is about daily response.
    - D) Excuse all behaviour ← Wrong. We still hold boundaries; we do so with understanding.

12. A child's behaviour that looks "problematic" in school may have been:
    - A) A bad choice ← Wrong. It may have been a survival strategy elsewhere.
    - B) A survival strategy in an unsafe environment — adaptive, not pathological ← Correct. The nervous system adapts to survive.
    - C) Their parents' fault ← Wrong. We don't assign blame; we respond to need.
    - D) Something we can ignore ← Wrong. We respond to need and create safety.

**Section 7 — Felt safety**

13. "Felt safety" means:
    - A) The building is secure ← Wrong. That's physical safety only.
    - B) The child feels emotionally safe enough to learn, connect, and make mistakes ← Correct. Physical safety isn't enough.
    - C) The child says they feel safe ← Wrong. They may not have the words or trust to say; we create conditions.
    - D) We never do anything that might upset them ← Wrong. We reduce unnecessary threat; we don't remove all challenge.

14. We can support felt safety by:
    - A) Only by referring to therapy ← Wrong. We create conditions every day.
    - B) Predictability, repair after rupture, consistent presence, reducing threat ← Correct. Daily practice.
    - C) Giving no consequences ← Wrong. Predictable, fair consequences can support safety when relationship is maintained.
    - D) Ignoring behaviour ← Wrong. We respond to need and hold boundaries.

**Section 8 — Co-regulation**

15. "Co-regulation" means:
    - A) The adult controls the child's emotions ← Wrong. The adult's regulated state supports the child's regulation; we don't control.
    - B) The adult's regulated state supports the child's regulation ← Correct. Your calm supports their calm.
    - C) The child regulates the adult ← Wrong. It's the other way around in our role.
    - D) Both must be calm at the same time ← Wrong. The adult leads; the child follows when they can.

16. You cannot co-regulate a child if:
    - A) The child is too dysregulated ← Wrong. That's when they need co-regulation most.
    - B) You are dysregulated yourself ← Correct. Regulate yourself first; tag out if needed.
    - C) The child has trauma ← Wrong. Trauma is exactly when co-regulation is needed.
    - D) You don't know their history ← Wrong. We don't need their history to offer calm presence.

**Section 9 — Secondary trauma**

17. "Vicarious trauma" or "secondary trauma" means:
    - A) The child's trauma is less important ← Wrong. It means staff can be affected by exposure to others' trauma.
    - B) Staff can be affected by repeated exposure to others' distress ← Correct. Your wellbeing matters; tag out, seek support.
    - C) Only therapists get it ← Wrong. Anyone repeatedly exposed can be affected.
    - D) We should avoid working with traumatised children ← Wrong. We support each other and protect capacity so we can stay in the work.

18. When you notice you're taking behaviour personally or becoming rigid:
    - A) Push through — the child needs you ← Wrong. Dysregulated adults can't co-regulate; you may escalate.
    - B) Pause, breathe, tag out if you can ← Correct. Your regulation supports theirs.
    - C) Apply firmer consequences ← Wrong. That's often your own dysregulation speaking.
    - D) Ignore it ← Wrong. Notice it and act to regulate.

**Section 10 — Care-experienced children**

19. For care-experienced children, school may be:
    - A) Just like for everyone else ← Wrong. Attachment disruption, loss, and the trauma of the care system itself affect how they experience school.
    - B) A place where attachment, loss, and instability outside school shape their need for predictability and safe relationships ← Correct. Prioritise felt safety and consistency.
    - C) Somewhere they don't need extra support ← Wrong. They often need predictable, relational safety above all.
    - D) Not our concern — that's social care ← Wrong. School is a key environment for safety and learning; we have a role.

20. When a care-experienced child deteriorates after contact visits:
    - A) That's not our business ← Wrong. It affects their capacity in school; we respond with support.
    - B) Flag to social worker and respond with extra predictability and support in school ← Correct. We don't investigate; we support and liaise.
    - C) Punish them for being unsettled ← Wrong. They need safety and regulation support, not consequence.
    - D) Assume they'll get over it ← Wrong. We document, support, and liaise with those who need to know.
