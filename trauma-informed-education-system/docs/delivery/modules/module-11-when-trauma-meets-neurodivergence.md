# Module 11: When Trauma Meets Neurodivergence

## Purpose

This module integrates the two default lenses: trauma and neurodivergence. It helps staff understand why these overlap in practice, why single-lens thinking fails (attributing everything to "just their autism" or "just their background"), and how to build responses that account for both. It emphasises the Matching Principle: support must match the child's neurology and history, not the system's default assumptions.

## Intended Audience

All staff who have completed or are familiar with Module 9 (Neurodivergence) and Module 10 (Trauma-Informed Practice), or equivalent foundations.

## Time Range

45–60 minutes (flexible)

## What Participants Should Leave With

1. **Why they overlap** — Neurodivergent children often accumulate trauma from being ND in a neurotypical world (rejection, punishment, masking exhaustion). Trauma and ND also present with overlapping behaviours (hypervigilance vs ADHD, freeze vs selective mutism).
2. **Diagnostic overshadowing** — When one label hides the other. Care-experienced children: higher rates of undiagnosed ND. ND children: more vulnerable to abuse; don't attribute everything to "just their autism."
3. **The behaviour plan trap** — Standard reward/consequence plans assume the child can regulate, predict consequences, be motivated by adult approval, and process verbal instructions in real time. Trauma and ND each undermine these; together they make such plans irrelevant or harmful.
4. **"Just their autism" / "Just their background"** — The danger of single-story thinking. Always ask: could this be both?
5. **The Matching Principle (Ward, 1998)** — The support we give practitioners should mirror the support we expect them to give children. The plan must match the child.
6. **Building support plans that account for BOTH** — Felt safety + environmental adjustment; co-regulation first; reduce sensory and emotional load; predictability for both ND and trauma.
7. **Working with families who are themselves ND and/or traumatised** — Parents may be undiagnosed or struggling; sensitivity in communication and expectations.
8. **Restraint, exclusion, and the school-to-prison pipeline** — Data on who is excluded; exhaustion of sensory, environmental, and relational approaches before restraint.
9. **Case studies** — Scenarios where dual-lens thinking changed outcomes (e.g. meltdown vs tantrum; PDA and consequences; LD + trauma and reward charts).
10. **Your role** — You don't need to be a psychologist; you need to be curious. Meet the nervous system where it is. Safety first. Curiosity second. Consequences last — if at all.

## Session Shape

### 45-Minute Variant

**Reading and orientation (25 minutes):**
- Why trauma and ND overlap; diagnostic overshadowing
- Behaviour plan trap; Matching Principle
- "If in doubt, respond to the nervous system state first, investigate causes second"

**Reflection and application (20 minutes):**
- Optional: one scenario where you might have attributed behaviour to one lens only — what would the other lens add?
- No personal disclosure; no naming children

### 60-Minute Variant

**Reading and orientation (35 minutes):**
- All sections 1–10
- Case studies; restraint and exclusion; working with families

**Reflection and application (25 minutes):**
- Optional: plan one change to your environment or response that would support both ND and trauma (e.g. predictability, low-demand high-warmth, co-regulate first)
- Quiz-style: "This behaviour has been attributed to 'just their autism.' What might we be missing?" (trauma lens)

## Optional Activities

### Activity 1: Dual-Lens Re-Read (5 minutes)

**Task:** Take a scenario (from training or anonymised) where behaviour was explained by one lens. List two possible contributions from the other lens. What would you do differently?

### Activity 2: Plan Mismatch (5 minutes)

**Task:** A child has a behaviour plan with rewards and consequences. It's been followed consistently for months. Behaviour has worsened. What might be wrong? (Plan may not match neurology or trauma; reward charts assume working memory, delayed gratification, adult approval — often not present for LD + trauma.)

### Activity 3: One Change (5 minutes)

**Task:** Identify one change that would support both neurodivergent and traumatised children in your setting (e.g. predictable transition, quiet space, processing time, reduced demand in crisis).

## Boundaries

- This module does **not** train staff to diagnose or to treat. It supports dual-lens thinking and system design.
- Safeguarding: disclosure of abuse or neglect → immediate DSL referral. Behaviour attributed to ND or background does **not** override safeguarding concern.
- Restraint: always ask whether sensory, environmental, and relational approaches have been exhausted first.

## Quiz

**Section 1 — Why they overlap**

1. Neurodivergent children often accumulate trauma because:
   - A) They are more prone to trauma biologically ← Wrong. They're more exposed to rejection, punishment, and misunderstanding in a neurotypical world.
   - B) They experience rejection, punishment for being themselves, sensory pain ignored, masking exhaustion ← Correct. The world isn't built for them; that experience is traumatic.
   - C) They have worse parents ← Wrong. We don't blame; we recognise systemic and relational factors.
   - D) Trauma and neurodivergence are the same thing ← Wrong. They're different; they overlap and interact.

2. Trauma responses and neurodivergent presentations can look identical, so:
   - A) We should pick one label and stick to it ← Wrong. We consider both; the child may have both.
   - B) We consider BOTH lenses and respond to nervous system state first ← Correct. Don't attribute everything to one.
   - C) We wait for a diagnosis before responding ← Wrong. We respond to need now; assessment can follow.
   - D) Only a psychologist can help ← Wrong. We create safety and environment; we refer when appropriate.

**Section 2 — Diagnostic overshadowing**

3. "It's just his autism" is a problem because:
   - A) It implies autism is bad ← Partially, but not the main issue.
   - B) It stops professionals looking for other explanations, including trauma ← Correct. One label absorbs all behaviour; the child can be autistic AND traumatised.
   - C) It's factually incorrect ← Not necessarily — the child may be autistic. The problem is using one lens to explain everything.
   - D) Parents don't like hearing it ← Not the core issue.

4. "It's just their background" (e.g. care-experienced) is a problem because:
   - A) Background doesn't matter ← Wrong. It does; but it's not the only lens.
   - B) Care-experienced children have significantly higher rates of undiagnosed neurodivergence — we may miss ND ← Correct. Single-story attribution misses need.
   - C) We shouldn't consider background ← Wrong. We consider both background and neurodivergence.
   - D) Only social workers should consider it ← Wrong. School has a role in both trauma and ND lens.

**Section 3 — Behaviour plan trap**

5. Traditional behaviour plans (rewards/consequences) often fail for children with BOTH trauma and neurodivergence because they assume the child can:
   - A) Read and write ← Wrong. The assumptions are about regulation, consequences, motivation, and processing.
   - B) Regulate their nervous system, predict consequences, be motivated by adult approval, and process verbal instructions in real time ← Correct. Trauma and ND each undermine these; together they make the plan irrelevant.
   - C) Behave if they want to ← Wrong. "Want to" ignores neurology and trauma.
   - D) Learn from punishment ← Wrong. Many of these children cannot learn from consequence in the way the plan assumes.

6. When a behaviour plan has been "followed consistently" but behaviour has worsened:
   - A) The plan needs to be stricter ← Wrong. The plan may not match the child's neurology or trauma.
   - B) The plan may not match the child — review with dual lens and change the approach ← Correct. Doing the wrong thing more consistently doesn't work.
   - C) The child is beyond help ← Wrong. The approach is wrong, not the child.
   - D) We need more staff to enforce it ← Wrong. Enforcement isn't the answer; match the plan to the child.

**Section 4 — Just their autism / just their background**

7. Attributing everything to "just their autism":
   - A) Is usually correct ← Wrong. It can hide trauma, other needs, or the interaction of both.
   - B) Can hide trauma and other needs — neurodivergent children are MORE vulnerable to abuse, not less ← Correct. Stay curious.
   - C) Is what parents want ← Wrong. Parents often want the full picture too.
   - D) Simplifies our response ← Wrong. Oversimplification leads to wrong response.

8. Attributing everything to "just their background":
   - A) Shows we're being sensitive ← Wrong. It can hide undiagnosed neurodivergence.
   - B) Can hide undiagnosed neurodivergence — care-experienced children have higher rates of ND ← Correct. Dual lens always.
   - C) Is required for safeguarding ← Wrong. Safeguarding considers context; it doesn't mean we ignore ND.
   - D) Doesn't affect the support we offer ← Wrong. Wrong attribution leads to wrong support.

**Section 5 — Matching Principle**

9. The Matching Principle (Ward, 1998) says:
   - A) We match children to the right school ← Wrong. It's about the support we give practitioners.
   - B) The support we give practitioners should mirror the support we expect them to give children ← Correct. We model the quality we want.
   - C) We match consequences to the behaviour ← Wrong. It's about system design, not behaviour management.
   - D) We match our response to the child's age ← Wrong. We match to nervous system state and need.

10. "The plan must match the child" means:
    - A) The child must fit the plan ← Wrong. The plan fits the child.
    - B) The plan should be built around how the child's brain works and what they've experienced ← Correct. Not how we wish their brain worked.
    - C) We need a different plan for every child ← Not necessarily; many plans can share principles but must be tailored.
    - D) Only specialists can design plans ← Wrong. We design with dual lens and involve specialists when needed.

**Section 6 — Building support plans**

11. A support plan that accounts for BOTH trauma and neurodivergence should prioritise:
    - A) Consequences first ← Wrong. Safety and regulation first.
    - B) Felt safety, co-regulation, reduced demand, predictability, and the child's interests ← Correct. Then build from there.
    - C) Compliance above all ← Wrong. Compliance without safety is meaningless for many of these children.
    - D) Referral out so we don't have to plan ← Wrong. We plan; we refer when appropriate alongside.

12. "Co-regulate first, investigate second" means:
    - A) We never investigate ← Wrong. We investigate when the child is calm and we can plan.
    - B) Meet the child's nervous system where it is first; understanding and assessment can follow ← Correct. You can't assess or reason when they're in survival mode.
    - C) We leave it to the therapist ← Wrong. We do the first response; we refer as needed.
    - D) We ignore the cause ← Wrong. We respond to state first; cause informs next steps when we can think clearly.

**Section 7 — Working with families**

13. Parents who are themselves neurodivergent or traumatised:
    - A) Are the cause of the child's problems ← Wrong. We don't blame; we consider the family system.
    - B) May need sensitivity in communication and expectations — the family system matters ← Correct. We don't pathologise; we adapt how we work.
    - C) Should be referred to therapy before we work with the child ← Wrong. We work with the child and family with sensitivity; refer when appropriate.
    - D) Don't need to be involved ← Wrong. Partnership with parents is important; we adapt our approach.

14. When sharing concerns with parents about possible neurodivergence or trauma:
    - A) We diagnose and tell them ← Wrong. We don't diagnose; we describe what we see and suggest next steps (e.g. GP, EP).
    - B) We describe what we see, offer curiosity not labels, and suggest they may wish to seek assessment ← Correct. Sensitive, non-diagnostic, supportive.
    - C) We don't mention it — that's for doctors ← Wrong. We can share observations and suggest pathways.
    - D) We insist they get an assessment ← Wrong. We suggest; we don't insist.

**Section 8 — Restraint and exclusion**

15. Before using restraint, we should:
    - A) Follow the policy ← Wrong. Policy may allow restraint; we should still ask whether we've exhausted other options first.
    - B) Ask: have we exhausted sensory, environmental, and relational approaches first? ← Correct. Restraint is last resort; dual lens may reveal what we haven't tried.
    - C) Get parent consent ← Wrong. That's not the first question; safety and alternatives are.
    - D) Only use it for aggression ← Wrong. The question is whether we've tried everything else first.

16. Neurodivergent and traumatised children are over-represented in exclusion data because:
    - A) They behave worse ← Wrong. Behaviour is communication; the system often doesn't match their needs.
    - B) Behaviour is often misread and support doesn't match need — system and lens matter ← Correct. We change our lens and our systems.
    - C) Their parents don't support the school ← Wrong. We don't blame; we improve our practice.
    - D) There's nothing we can do ← Wrong. Dual lens and matching support can reduce exclusion.

**Section 9 — Case studies**

17. In a "meltdown misread as tantrum" scenario, the key is:
    - A) Apply the behaviour policy ← Wrong. Meltdown isn't tantrum; policy may be wrong for this moment.
    - B) Treat as possible meltdown: safety, reduce sensory load, no reasoning mid-crisis ← Correct. Then review and refer as needed.
    - C) Ignore until they stop ← Wrong. Ignoring a meltdown increases distress.
    - D) Send home ← Wrong. That may be needed sometimes, but first response is safety and regulation.

18. When a reward chart "hasn't worked" for a child with LD and trauma:
    - A) The child needs to try harder ← Wrong. The tool doesn't match the child.
    - B) The chart assumes working memory, delayed gratification, and adult approval — often not present; redesign with felt safety and co-regulation ← Correct. Match the plan to the child.
    - C) We need a bigger reward ← Wrong. The mechanism is wrong, not the size of the reward.
    - D) The child is beyond behavioural support ← Wrong. The type of support is wrong.

**Section 10 — Your role**

19. You don't need to be a psychologist to use the dual lens; you need to:
    - A) Diagnose the child ← Wrong. We don't diagnose.
    - B) Be curious, meet the nervous system where it is, put safety first and consequences last ← Correct. That's within our role.
    - C) Refer everything out ← Wrong. We respond first; we refer when appropriate.
    - D) Follow the behaviour policy exactly ← Wrong. Policy may need to be applied with dual lens; sometimes the policy doesn't match the child.

20. "If in doubt, respond to the nervous system state first, investigate causes second" means:
    - A) We never investigate ← Wrong. We investigate when we can.
    - B) In the moment, reduce demand, increase safety, co-regulate; understanding and assessment can follow when the child is calm ← Correct. You can't do good assessment in crisis.
    - C) We leave investigation to parents ← Wrong. We're part of the picture; we document and refer as needed.
    - D) We ignore causes ← Wrong. We prioritise state first; causes inform next steps.
